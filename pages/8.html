There is a red bucket that lives in my grandparents' garage, on Stockley street,

five and a half blocks away from the beach
and two and a half blocks away from the park with the duck pond.

It is the white house with the wicker chairs on the porch
and a big fern in the right corner.

It is the house filled with decades worth of seashells and sand dollars.

They live in bowls and vases, on lampshades, on tables, and on walls.

My granddad always brought the bucket to the beach.
He would tell me to close my eyes and count to thirty,
so that he could get a head start.
Then he would run ahead making sandcastles as fast as he could, forming a trail of packed piles of sand right behind him.

The sandcastles were not for looking at though,
they were for destroying.

When I would reach thirty seconds
I would open my eyes to him frantically building.
It will always bring a smile to my face.
I would run to chase the trail he had made,
stomping on each one as he tried to keep up,
trying to keep the production ahead of my wrath.

I always caught up to him.

He is a lover of laughter, he was always closer to my age then his own.
